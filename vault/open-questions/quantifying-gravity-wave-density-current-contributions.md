title: Gravity Wave vs Density Current Contributions
---

**Background:** The offshore propagation of diurnal convection, a phenomenon accounting for significant tropical rainfall, is driven by multiple physical mechanisms, most notably atmospheric gravity waves and density currents. Current atmospheric models often struggle to separate these influences, leading to errors in reproducing the diurnal cycle and its long-range offshore extension.

**Question / Future Work:** Develop observational and numerical frameworks to quantify the relative contributions of gravity waves versus density currents in driving convective propagation across varied synoptic, environmental, and topographical conditions. Determining the dominant mechanism and their interaction under differing sea surface temperatures and background wind regimes is critical for improving precipitation parameterization in global climate models.

**Why It Matters:** Establishing the dominance and interaction of these physical drivers is a fundamental bottleneck for accurate, physics-informed precipitation forecasting in tropical regions like the Maritime Continent.

**Evidence:** Future work will quantify the relative contributions of gravity waves and density currents under a range of synoptic and large-scale conditions and assess how the atmospheric and oceanic boundary layers interact under varying SSTs and wind regimes.
