title: Causal Identification of Reflexivity
---

**Background:** The reflexivity thesis suggests that market probabilities can become self-fulfilling or self-defeating by organizing collective behavior, yet causal identification remains difficult due to the presence of multiple confounding informational signals. Existing research lacks the longitudinal or structural data to isolate the marginal contribution of market signals from other factors like media coverage and polls.

**Question / Future Work:** Causal identification of the reflexive channel requires isolating the marginal effect of prediction market signals from other influential factors such as polling data, party internal information, and media narratives. Standard observational data is insufficient for this task, as these factors move concurrently. Future research must develop natural experiments or structural identification strategies to quantify whether market probabilities truly drive behavioral change or simply correlate with it.

**Why It Matters:** Quantifying this effect is necessary to move beyond conditional claims and establish whether prediction markets act as significant drivers or merely passive indicators in political and economic systems.

**Evidence:** Disentangling the marginal contribution of prediction market prices from this broader informational environment requires either natural experiments ... or structural identification strategies that are not yet available in the existing literature. The magnitude of the effect remains an open empirical question.
