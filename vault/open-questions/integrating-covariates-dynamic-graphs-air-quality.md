title: Integrating Covariates in Air Quality Forecasting
---

**Background:** Spatiotemporal air quality forecasting models often rely on historical pollutant concentration data and fixed distance-based graph structures. Integrating auxiliary variables such as meteorology, traffic, and emission sources remains a significant challenge for improving predictive accuracy in complex urban environments.

**Question / Future Work:** There is an unresolved need to extend graph convolutional support vector regression (GCSVR) frameworks by incorporating domain-informed covariates like boundary-layer height, wind patterns, and vehicular or industrial emission proxies, as well as developing dynamic or causally informed graph structures to represent shifting pollutant transport mechanisms.

**Why It Matters:** Integrating external environmental factors is critical for transitioning from purely data-driven models to more physically consistent and accurate forecasting systems, particularly in regions where meteorological variability dominates pollution dispersion.

**Evidence:** Future work can extend GCSVR by integrating domain-informed covariates such as boundary-layer height, wind speed and direction, temperature, humidity, vehicular emissions, industrial activity, and biomass-burning indicators. Dynamic or causally informed graph structures could further improve the model’s ability to represent changing pollutant transport mechanisms across seasons and cities.
