title: Limitations of aggregate counterfactual forecasting
---

**Background:** Urban mobility interventions often generate complex, system-wide spillover effects that complicate the construction of traditional control groups for causal evaluation. The validity of using forecasting models to generate counterfactuals for such policy interventions is constrained by potential contamination from unobserved contemporaneous shocks.

**Question / Future Work:** Research is needed to quantify the limitations of counterfactual forecasting when isolating intervention effects from concurrent exogenous shocks, and to determine how to extend this methodology to distinguish individual-level behaviors—such as mode switching, re-routing, and trip cancellation—from aggregate ridership patterns. Furthermore, it is essential to establish whether short-term policy impacts persist or evolve as user adaptation scales over longer durations.

**Why It Matters:** This identifies a critical bottleneck in the reliability of observational causal inference for urban policy evaluation, distinguishing between statistical forecasting performance and policy impact validity.

**Evidence:** First, forecast-based counterfactuals cannot fully rule out contemporaneous shocks... Second, aggregate datasets cannot directly identify individual mode switching... Fourth, the current analysis focuses on short-term responses... longer-term evaluation is needed.
