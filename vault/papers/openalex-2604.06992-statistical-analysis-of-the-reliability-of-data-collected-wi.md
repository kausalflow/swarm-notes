# Statistical Analysis of the Reliability of Data Collected with Wireless Electrocardiograms Outside Clinical Settings

**Authors**: Yalemzerf Getnet, Waltenegus Dargie
**Date**: 2026-04-08
**Paper ID**: [openalex:2604.06992](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.06992)

## Summary

This study evaluates the reliability of wireless electrocardiogram (ECG) data collected in non-clinical, unsupervised settings by comparing them against standard 12-lead and Holter ECG data. The researchers analyzed RR interval time series (tachograms) and heart rate variability (HRV) metrics from 54 healthy subjects performing various physical activities. Statistical analysis, including confidence intervals and p-value testing, reveals a high degree of agreement between wireless-acquired data and clinical benchmarks. These findings support the use of scalable, wireless ECG devices for reliable long-term cardiac monitoring and the study of cardiac workload in everyday environments.

## Key Contributions

- Provides a statistical validation of wireless ECG data collected in non-clinical, unsupervised settings against established 12-lead and Holter ECG clinical standards.
- Demonstrates through 95% confidence intervals and p-value hypothesis testing that RR interval and heart rate variability (HRV) metrics derived from portable wireless devices are statistically consistent with clinical benchmarks.
- Establishes the feasibility of utilizing cost-effective, wireless cardiac monitoring for long-term physiological studies outside controlled medical environments.
