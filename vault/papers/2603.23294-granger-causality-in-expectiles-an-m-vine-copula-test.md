# Granger Causality in Expectiles: an M-vine copula test

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel, model-free measure for Granger causality framed in terms of expectiles, which are theoretically desirable risk measures that allow for a deeper analysis of distributional dependence beyond mean-based causality. A corresponding statistical test is constructed based on M-vine copula models, enabling the non-parametric detection of multivariate Granger causality among $d+1$ time series under arbitrary non-linear and non-Gaussian dependence. The authors prove the strong consistency of this test statistic and show via simulations that it maintains accurate size control while offering superior power, especially in detecting complex joint causal structures. The framework's practical relevance is illustrated through applications to international stock market indices, confirming its ability to uncover relationships invisible to simpler pairwise analyses.

## Key Contributions

- Proposal of a model-free measure for Granger causality specifically defined in terms of expectiles, allowing for distributional causality analysis beyond the mean.
- Development of a non-parametric statistical test for multivariate Granger causality involving $d+1$ series, utilizing M-vine copulas to handle non-linear and non-Gaussian dependence structures.
- Establishment of the strong consistency of the proposed test statistic under general regularity conditions.
- Demonstration of the test's capability to detect multivariate causal relationships that are missed by traditional pairwise causality tests.

## Key Concepts

- [Granger Causality in Expectiles](../concepts/granger-causality-in-expectiles.md): A model-free measure of Granger causality generalized to arbitrary quantiles (expectiles) of the conditional distribution.
- [M-vine Copula Test](../concepts/m-vine-copula-test.md): A non-parametric statistical test for multivariate Granger causality built upon the M-vine copula structure to model complex dependencies.

## Limitations

The strong consistency proof relies on specific, though general, regularity conditions that would need to be verified for real-world data. The method's performance on extremely high-dimensional settings beyond the $d+1$ series considered is not explicitly detailed.
