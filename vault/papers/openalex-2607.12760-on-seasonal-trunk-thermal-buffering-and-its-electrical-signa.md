# On seasonal trunk thermal buffering and its electrical signature in mature trees

**Authors**: Jean-Baptiste Boulé, Vincent Courtillot, Dominique Gibert, Fernando Lopes, Alexis Maineult, Pierpaolo Zuddas, Jean de Bremond d'Ars
**Date**: 2026-07-14
**Paper ID**: [openalex:2607.12760](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12760)

## Summary

This study investigates the thermal buffering mechanisms of mature tree trunks by analyzing continuous multi-year measurements of temperature and spontaneous electrical potential (SP). The findings suggest that tree trunks are not merely passive thermal storage units but utilize hydraulic sap transport to regulate internal temperatures against atmospheric variability. By applying phase-space analysis and an energy-balance model, the authors quantify a consistent relationship between SP and trunk-soil temperature differences, revealing a ~100-day lag in thermo-hydraulic regulation. This research introduces a non-invasive methodology for monitoring long-term physiological responses in trees to climatic seasonal shifts.

## Key Contributions

- Demonstrates that mature tree trunks exhibit thermal buffering driven by vertically mediated, hydraulic heat transfer, rather than simple heat storage.
- Identifies spontaneous electrical potential as a non-invasive indicator for long-term tree hydraulic and thermal processes.
- Provides a minimal energy-balance model confirming that trunk heat storage alone cannot explain observed seasonal temperature dynamics.

## Archivist Review

The approved concepts and open question address a distinct, reusable physiological phenomenon (thermo-hydraulic coupling) that challenges traditional thermal models of trees. I rejected the SP indicator concept name in favor of a more generalized proxy nomenclature. The review followed the policy of being selective while preserving the cross-disciplinary importance of the study for long-term physiological modeling.

### Approved Concepts
- Thermo-hydraulic coupling in tree trunks: Identifies a mechanism linking hydraulic transport to thermal buffering, providing a basis for understanding tree physiological resilience.
- Spontaneous electrical potential as a physiological proxy: Establishes a novel, non-invasive method for monitoring slow-scale physiological processes in trees using spontaneous electrical potential.

### Approved Open Questions
- Mechanisms of Trunk Thermo-hydraulic Coupling: Addressing this is critical to move from correlative evidence of thermo-hydraulic coupling to a mechanistic understanding of internal temperature regulation in response to climate fluctuations.
