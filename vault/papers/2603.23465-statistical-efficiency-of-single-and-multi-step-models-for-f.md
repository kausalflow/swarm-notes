# Statistical Efficiency of Single- and Multi-step Models for Forecasting and Control

**Authors**: Anne Somalwar, Bruce D. Lee, George J. Pappas, Nikolai Matni
**Date**: 2026-03-24
**ArXiv**: [2603.23465](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.23465)

## Summary

This work analyzes the fundamental trade-off between using single-step versus multi-step predictors to mitigate compounding error in learning-based control for linear dynamical systems. The authors theoretically and empirically compare three modeling approaches: direct single-step prediction, direct multi-step prediction, and single-step models trained with multi-step loss functions. They establish that when system dynamics are perfectly captured (well-specified models), single-step methods yield the best asymptotic prediction error. Conversely, when models suffer from model-class misspecification due to unobserved system states (partial observability), direct multi-step predictors offer substantial improvements in accuracy by reducing accumulated bias. These fundamental performance differences are shown to translate directly to closed-loop control performance.

## Key Contributions

- Provided the first quantitative analysis of the trade-off between single-step and multi-step forecasting models for linear dynamical systems.
- Demonstrated that well-specified single-step models achieve the lowest asymptotic prediction error under ideal conditions.
- Showed that direct multi-step predictors significantly reduce bias and improve accuracy when the model class is misspecified due to partial observability.
- Provided theoretical and empirical evidence that these trade-offs in prediction error persist when models are applied in closed-loop control settings.

## Limitations

The primary analysis is focused on linear dynamical systems, and the findings may not directly generalize to highly nonlinear systems without further study.
