# Statistical Efficiency of Single- and Multi-step Models for Forecasting and Control

## Summary

This work analyzes the statistical efficiency trade-off between using sequentially rolled-out single-step models versus directly trained multi-step predictors for forecasting and control, particularly in the context of compounding error. The authors theoretically and empirically show that when system dynamics are perfectly captured (well-specified), single-step models achieve the minimal asymptotic prediction error. Conversely, if the system is partially observable, leading to model misspecification, direct multi-step predictors can substantially mitigate bias and outperform single-step rollouts. These findings establish a clear principle for model selection in time-series applications, with implications for closed-loop control performance.

## Key Contributions

- Provided the first quantitative analysis of the trade-off between single-step and multi-step predictors for forecasting and control in linear dynamical systems.
- Demonstrated that well-specified single-step models achieve the lowest asymptotic prediction error compared to multi-step models.
- Showed that direct multi-step predictors significantly reduce bias and improve accuracy when the model class suffers from partial observability (model misspecification).
- Provided theoretical and empirical evidence that the observed trade-offs between predictor types persist when used in closed-loop control settings.

## Key Concepts

- [Compounding Error](../concepts/compounding-error.md): The accumulation of small prediction errors over successive time steps, which is a major challenge in time-series prediction and control.

## Limitations

The analysis is theoretically grounded in linear dynamical systems, and the results' extension to highly nonlinear systems requires further investigation.
