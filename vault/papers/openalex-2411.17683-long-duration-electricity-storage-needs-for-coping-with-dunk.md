# Long-duration electricity storage needs for coping with Dunkelflaute events in Europe

**Authors**: Martin Kittel, Alexander Roth, Wolf-Peter Schill
**Date**: 2026-05-09
**Paper ID**: [openalex:2411.17683](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.17683)

## Summary

This paper investigates the infrastructure requirements for balancing European power systems during 'Dunkelflaute' events—prolonged periods of low wind and solar energy availability. By analyzing 35 years of historical meteorological data, the authors demonstrate that the system's least-cost configuration for resilience against extreme droughts necessitates massive deployment of long-duration energy storage. The study finds that alternative mitigation strategies, such as nuclear power or carbon-captured fossil backup, provide only limited relief compared to the required storage expansion. These results suggest that long-duration storage is essential for safeguarding energy transition pathways in highly renewable electricity systems.

## Key Contributions

- Quantifies the necessity of 351 terawatt hours of long-duration storage capacity to reliably manage extreme 'Dunkelflaute' renewable energy droughts in a decarbonized European power sector.
- Demonstrates through 35 years of historical weather data that extreme renewable droughts, rather than average conditions, dictate the optimal capacity and operation of long-duration electricity storage.
- Evaluates the comparative impact of nuclear power and fossil backup with carbon removal on reducing long-duration storage requirements in a renewable-integrated European grid.
