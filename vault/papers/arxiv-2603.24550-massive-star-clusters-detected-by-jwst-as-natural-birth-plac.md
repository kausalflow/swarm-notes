# Massive star clusters detected by JWST as natural birth places to form intermediate-mass black holes

**Authors**: Dominik R. G. Schleicher, Matías Liempi, Mirek Giersz, Marcelo C. Vergara, Francesco Flammini Dotti, Paulo Solar, Andrés Escala, Muhammad A. Latif, Bastián Reinoso, Abbas Askar, Raffaella Schneider, Roberto Capuzzo-Dolcetta, Jorge Saavedra-Bastidas, Fernando Cuevas
**Date**: 2026-03-25
**Paper ID**: [arxiv:2603.24550](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.24550)

## Summary

This work investigates whether Young Massive Star Clusters (YMCs) detected by JWST act as birthplaces for intermediate-mass black holes (IMBHs). The authors compare the population properties of these YMCs, particularly their mass-radius relation and short dynamical timescales, to theoretical expectations for runaway collision-based IMBH formation. They quantitatively estimate that approximately 16% of YMCs, those beyond the $1\sigma$ scatter in the mass-radius relation, are likely candidates to form IMBHs. Furthermore, clusters exceeding $\sim 6 \times 10^6 M_\odot$ are predicted to retain gas, accelerating central object growth through dynamical friction and Bondi accretion, while also considering a gas-dominated channel for direct high-mass black hole formation.

## Key Contributions

- Established that a significant fraction of JWST-detected Young Massive Star Clusters (YMCs) are candidates for forming intermediate-mass black holes (IMBHs) via runaway collision channels.
- Quantified the required cluster size ($\gtrsim 1\sigma$ scatter in the mass-radius relation) necessary for efficient IMBH formation via runaway collisions.
- Demonstrated that YMCs above $\sim 6 \times 10^6 M_\odot$ likely retain gas despite supernova feedback, facilitating rapid IMBH growth via gas friction and Bondi accretion.
- Investigated the alternative scenario where gas-dominated regimes in YMCs could lead to the direct formation of high-mass black holes via strong gravitational torques.

## Limitations

The analysis relies on theoretical modeling of cluster evolution and comparisons with local/Milky Way clusters, and the JWST observations primarily provide lensing signatures, requiring subsequent dynamical modeling. The specific observational confirmation of IMBH formation within these YMCs remains a future step.
