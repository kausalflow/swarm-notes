# Cluster gravitational redshifts: uncertainties and survey requirements

**Authors**: Eleni Tsaprazi, Giorgio F. Lesci, Federico Marulli, Alan F. Heavens, Enrico Maraboli
**Date**: 2026-03-26
**ArXiv**: [2603.25339](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.25339)

## Summary

This work develops an end-to-end forecasting pipeline to rigorously assess how observational and theoretical uncertainties impact the precision of constraining modified gravity ($\alpha_\mathrm{MG}$) using the gravitational redshift effect in galaxy clusters. The methodology involves generating synthetic halo catalogs using a Halo Occupation Distribution (HOD) model, simulating survey selections, and parameterizing deviations from General Relativity via a linear rescaling of the gravitational potential. Key findings indicate that velocity dispersion sets a precision floor for redshift uncertainty, and that accurate cluster center determination is paramount to avoid misinterpreting systematic errors as new physics in upcoming high-S/N surveys. Ultimately, the study suggests that high-precision spectroscopic surveys are the most effective path for leveraging gravitational redshifts as a competitive probe of modified gravity.

## Key Contributions

- Developed an end-to-end forecasting pipeline to quantify the impact of observational and theoretical uncertainties on constraining modified gravity ($\alpha_\mathrm{MG}$) using cluster gravitational redshifts.
- Determined that the intracluster velocity dispersion imposes an effective floor, where redshift precision beyond $\sigma_z\sim 10^{-4}(1+z)$ yields no improvement in $\alpha_\mathrm{MG}$ precision.
- Found that shallow, narrower spectroscopic surveys are superior to deep, wide photometric surveys for achieving precise constraints on modified gravity parameters.
- Identified that accurate determination of cluster centers is essential in high signal-to-noise ratio (S/N) regimes, as mis-centering can introduce biases mimicking significant deviations from General Relativity (GR).

## Limitations

The study focuses on specific observational uncertainties and theoretical modeling choices for galaxy clusters; the general applicability to other cosmological probes might differ.
