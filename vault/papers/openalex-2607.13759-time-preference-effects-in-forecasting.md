# Time preference effects in forecasting

**Authors**: Yannick Hoga, Niklas V. Lehmann
**Date**: 2026-07-15
**Paper ID**: [openalex:2607.13759](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13759)

## Summary

This paper investigates how the timing of reward evaluation for uncertain future events (e.g., recessions, eruptions) influences forecaster behavior. The authors theoretically demonstrate that retrospective evaluation incentivizes agents to distort probabilities toward earlier event occurrences when they prioritize immediate rewards. They confirm these theoretical findings through an analysis of forecasting tournament data, revealing a systematic bias toward early-event overestimation. The work concludes that achieving incentive-compatible reward structures for such longitudinal event forecasting remains a fundamental challenge in decision science.

## Key Contributions

- Demonstrates that evaluating event forecasts retrospectively creates incentives for forecasters to dishonestly accelerate predicted occurrence dates.
- Provides empirical evidence from forecasting tournament data showing systematic overstatement of early-event probabilities due to time-preference incentives.
- Proves that designing incentive-compatible reward mechanisms for event-timing forecasts is fundamentally challenging.
