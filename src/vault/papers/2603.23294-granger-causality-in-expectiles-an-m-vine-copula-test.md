# Granger Causality in Expectiles: an M-vine copula test

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel, model-free measure for Granger causality operating in the space of expectiles, extending the concept beyond the mean to study distributional causality at any point of the conditional distribution. To test for multivariate Granger causality involving $d+1$ series under general non-linear and non-Gaussian dependency, the authors develop a test statistic founded on M-vine copula models, thereby avoiding parametric assumptions on the underlying joint distribution. The strong consistency of this test statistic is proven, and simulations confirm accurate size control and increasing power with sample size. A significant advantage of this framework is its capacity for joint hypothesis testing, successfully identifying causal structures invisible to standard pairwise analyses.

## Key Contributions

- Proposing a model-free measure for Granger causality based on expectiles, allowing the study of distributional causality at arbitrary positions of the conditional distribution.
- Developing a multivariate Granger causality test using M-vine copula models that is robust to non-linear and non-Gaussian dependence structures without imposing parametric assumptions on the joint distribution.
- Establishing the strong consistency of the proposed test statistic under stated regularity conditions.
- Demonstrating the ability of the test to detect joint causal relationships that are missed by traditional pairwise tests, both theoretically and empirically.

## Key Concepts

- [Granger Causality in Expectiles](../concepts/granger-causality-in-expectiles.md): A model-free measure of Granger causality generalized from the traditional mean-based measure to arbitrary positions of the conditional distribution using expectiles.
- [M-vine Copula Test](../concepts/m-vine-copula-test.md): A test for multivariate Granger causality in expectiles that leverages M-vine copula models to handle non-linear and non-Gaussian dependence without parametric assumptions.

## Limitations

The strong consistency of the test statistic is established only under certain regularity conditions, which may restrict applicability in highly irregular data generating processes.
